The Chinese Calendar

The lunisolar system and sexagenary cycle that have measured time in China for over three millennia

Introduction to the Chinese Calendar

The Chinese calendar is a lunisolar system that tracks both the moon's phases and the sun's position. Unlike the purely solar Gregorian calendar, it incorporates lunar months (new moon to new moon) while using solar markers to stay aligned with the seasons. This dual nature made it indispensable for agriculture, festival scheduling, astrological calculation, and daily planning for thousands of years.

The calendar operates on two parallel tracks: the lunar month system governing festivals and daily life, and the sexagenary cycle (干支) of Heavenly Stems and Earthly Branches governing astrological and cosmological reckoning. Together they form one of the most sophisticated timekeeping systems in human history.

Historical Origins

Evidence from Shang Dynasty oracle bones (c. 1600-1046 BCE) reveals calendar notations using the sexagenary cycle to record days. The system of pairing Heavenly Stems with Earthly Branches predates the zodiac animals and represents one of humanity's oldest continuous timekeeping methods.

By the Zhou Dynasty (1046-256 BCE), the calendar was sophisticated enough to predict eclipses and track the positions of major celestial bodies. Court astronomers maintained detailed records and the calendar carried political significance — the right to promulgate the calendar was a prerogative of the emperor, symbolising his mandate to order time itself.

The landmark reform came under Emperor Wu of Han in 104 BCE, establishing the Taichu (太初) calendar. This reform fixed the beginning of the year to the first day of the first lunar month (roughly corresponding to late January or February in the Gregorian calendar), a convention that persists today as the basis for Chinese New Year.

The Sexagenary Cycle (六十干支)

The sexagenary cycle creates 60 unique combinations by pairing Heavenly Stems with Earthly Branches. Because Yang stems only pair with Yang branches and Yin with Yin, the result is 60 (not 120) combinations. The cycle repeats every 60 years, months, days, and double-hours, providing a multi-layered temporal framework.

The first combination is Jiǎzǐ (甲子, Yang Wood Rat). The cycle proceeds: Yǐchǒu, Bǐngyín, Dīngmǎo... until reaching the 60th combination, Guǐhài (癸亥, Yin Water Pig), before restarting.

This 60-year cycle is the basis of the BaZi system and determines the precise elemental character of each year. For example, 2026 is Bǐngwǔ (丙午) — Yang Fire Horse — the 43rd combination in the current cycle.

Lunisolar Mechanics

Months follow lunar phases: each begins on a new moon and lasts 29 or 30 days (averaging 29.53 days). Twelve lunar months total roughly 354 days — about 11 days short of the solar year. To prevent the calendar from drifting through the seasons, intercalary months (閏月, rùnyuè) are inserted approximately every three years (seven times in nineteen years, following the Metonic cycle).

The solar component is tracked through the 24 Solar Terms (節氣, jiéqì), which divide the sun's annual path into 24 equal segments of roughly 15 days each. Solar Terms determine which month receives an intercalary insertion and anchor the calendar to the agricultural seasons.

The Calendar in Daily Life

For millennia the Chinese calendar governed the rhythm of daily existence. Farmers relied on Solar Terms to determine planting and harvest times. Wedding dates, funerals, business openings, and travel were all selected according to auspicious calendar calculations. The BaZi system draws its four pillars directly from the sexagenary cycle of the birth date and time. Feng shui practitioners use the calendar to calculate Flying Star periods and determine temporal energy shifts.

Frequently Asked Questions

The sexagenary cycle (六十干支) creates 60 unique combinations from 10 Heavenly Stems and 12 Earthly Branches. It repeats every 60 years and is used to count years, months, days, and hours in traditional Chinese timekeeping.

Chinese months follow lunar phases, alternating between 29 days (short month, 小月) and 30 days (long month, 大月), averaging 29.53 days per month.

The 24 Solar Terms (節氣) divide the sun's annual path into 24 segments of ~15 days each. They mark seasonal transitions and guide agricultural activities. They were inscribed as UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage in 2016.

Chinese New Year falls on the second new moon after the winter solstice, placing it between January 21 and February 20. The exact date shifts yearly because the lunisolar calendar must reconcile lunar months with the solar year.

A purely lunar calendar (like the Islamic Hijri calendar) tracks only moon phases and drifts through the seasons. The Chinese lunisolar calendar also tracks the sun's position through Solar Terms and uses intercalary months to stay aligned with the seasons.
