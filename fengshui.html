Feng Shui — The Art of Wind and Water

An ancient Chinese geomantic science for reading landscapes, assessing Qi flow, and harmonising people with their environment

What Is Feng Shui?

Feng Shui (風水, literally "wind-water") is the traditional Chinese system of geomancy — the art and science of assessing landscapes and dwellings to understand how natural energy (氣, Qi) flows through them. Its purpose is to identify locations where Qi gathers beneficially and to orient structures so inhabitants live in harmony with natural forces.

Classical Feng Shui is not interior decorating. It is an ancient and rigorous discipline of land assessment, rooted in millennia of empirical observation, cosmological theory, and scholarly refinement. The Zangshu (葬書, Book of Burial) states: "Qi rides the wind and scatters, but is retained when encountering water." This single principle gives Feng Shui its name and foundational logic.

Historical Origins

Feng Shui's roots stretch back over 3,500 years. Shang Dynasty (c. 1600-1046 BCE) burial sites show careful orientation according to cardinal directions and terrain, demonstrating that auspicious siting was already practised during China's earliest recorded civilisations.

The foundational text is the Zangshu, attributed to Guo Pu (郭璞) of the Jin Dynasty (266-420 CE). He systematised burial geomancy principles, articulating how landforms determine Qi flow.

During the Tang Dynasty, master Yang Yunsong (楊筠松) served as imperial geomancer before founding the Form School in Jiangxi. His texts — the Hanlongjing (撼龍經) and Yilongjing (疑龍經) — laid out precise methods for tracing dragon veins through mountain ranges.

The Song Dynasty saw the Compass School mature, with masters developing Flying Stars (Xuan Kong) and refining the Luopan. Feng Shui became integral to state planning: capitals like Chang'an and Luoyang were sited according to geomantic principles.

The Form School (巒頭派)

The Form School reads the physical landscape directly — mountains, rivers, terrain contours — to understand Qi movement. Central is the concept of mountains as dragons: ridges along which Qi flows are dragon veins (龍脈). A geomancer traces these from distant peaks to where Qi settles — the dragon's lair (龍穴).

Any site is assessed through the Four Celestial Animals:

  • Azure Dragon (青龍) — East: Hills to the left (facing south), slightly higher, representing protective yang energy.
  • White Tiger (白虎) — West: Terrain to the right, lower and subdued. If higher than the Dragon, the site is inauspicious.
  • Black Tortoise (玄武) — North: Mountain behind, providing solid backing and shielding from northern winds.
  • Vermilion Bird (朱雀) — South: Open space in front, allowing Qi to gather in a "bright hall" (明堂).

The Compass School (理氣派)

The Compass School introduces mathematical precision using the Luopan compass. It maps invisible energies of compass directions, trigram sectors, and temporal cycles.

The Eight Trigrams (八卦) form the backbone of directional analysis. Derived from the Yijing, the trigrams — Qian, Kun, Zhen, Xun, Kan, Li, Gen, Dui — each govern a direction with specific elemental and symbolic associations.

The most sophisticated method is Xuan Kong Feixing (玄空飛星, Flying Stars), which assigns shifting numerical values to directional sectors according to 20-year, annual, and monthly cycles. This allows practitioners to assess when a site's energies peak and decline.

The Luopan Compass

The Luopan (羅盤) is a portable encyclopaedia of Chinese cosmology. At its centre lies the Heaven Pool (天池) — a magnetic needle pointing to magnetic south. Radiating outward are concentric rings containing:

  • 24 Mountains (二十四山) — 24 sectors of 15 degrees each, combining Earthly Branches, Heavenly Stems, and trigrams.
  • Trigram rings — Earlier Heaven and Later Heaven arrangements for different analytical purposes.
  • Stems and Branches — Various combinations for temporal and directional calculations.
  • 28 Lunar Mansions — Correlating terrestrial directions with celestial constellations.

Feng Shui in Imperial China

The Forbidden City sits on a north-south axis with Jingshan Hill behind (Black Tortoise), Tiananmen Square to the south (bright hall), and the Golden Water River curving before the entrance in a jade-belt formation.

The Ming Tombs valley was selected for near-perfect geomantic configuration: a horseshoe of mountains on three sides with a watercourse flowing through the southern opening. Thirteen Ming emperors rest within this single embrace.

Chang'an was positioned on the Wei River plain backed by the Qinling Mountains; Luoyang sat at the confluence of the Luo and Yi rivers. These were systematic geomantic selections at the highest imperial level.

Five Elements in Feng Shui

The Five Elements provide the language for describing how Qi manifests. In the Form School, landscape shapes encode elements: pointed peaks are Fire, flat tops are Earth, rounded hills Metal, undulations Water, tall formations Wood.

In the Compass School, each trigram sector carries an element: Kan (north) is Water, Li (south) Fire, Zhen/Xun are Wood, Qian/Dui are Metal, Gen/Kun are Earth. The interaction between a building's direction and these sectors determines energy quality.

Classical vs. Modern Feng Shui

Classical Feng Shui (pre-Qing) requires years of training: physical site assessment, Luopan readings, Flying Star calculations, 24 mountains analysis, and dragon vein tracing. Practitioners were literate scholars integrating cosmology, astronomy, and geography.

Modern pop Feng Shui strips these foundations, reducing the discipline to generic rules divorced from compass measurement or landscape assessment. The Black Sect Tantric Buddhist (BTB) method — a modern invention with no classical Chinese lineage — is often confused with traditional geomancy.

The pre-Qing classical texts — from Guo Pu through Yang Yunsong to Song and Ming masters — represent a coherent, philosophically grounded body of knowledge. Serious Feng Shui study begins with these sources.
