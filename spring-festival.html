Spring Festival — Chinese New Year

The world's oldest continuous celebration, marking the turn of the lunisolar year since ancient times

TwitterFacebookLinkedInEmail

What Is the Spring Festival?

The Spring Festival (春節, Chūnjié), also known as Chinese New Year, marks the beginning of a new year on the traditional Chinese lunisolar calendar. It is the most important festival in Chinese culture, celebrated for at least 3,500 years, and the longest public holiday in China and many East Asian nations.

Ancient Origins

The festival's roots lie in ancient year-end sacrificial rites (臘祭, làjì) during the Shang Dynasty, when people offered sacrifices to ancestors and deities to give thanks for the harvest and pray for a prosperous new year. The word "Nian" (年) originally referred to the ripening of grain — a full harvest cycle.

By the Zhou Dynasty, formalised celebrations at year's end included communal feasting and ritual offerings. Emperor Wu of Han's calendar reform in 104 BCE fixed the new year to the first day of the first lunar month, establishing the date that has endured for over two thousand years.

The Legend of Nian (年獸)

According to folk tradition, the mythical beast Nian (年獸) would emerge from the sea or mountains on New Year's Eve to devour crops, livestock, and even children. The terrified villagers eventually discovered that the creature feared three things: the colour red, loud noises, and bright fire.

From this legend spring the quintessential New Year customs: hanging red decorations and couplets, setting off firecrackers and fireworks, and lighting bonfires and lanterns. While the legend's exact origins are debated, it reflects the fundamentally apotropaic (evil-averting) nature of Spring Festival traditions — the collective effort to drive away misfortune and welcome renewal.

The Fifteen Days of Celebration

  • New Year's Eve (除夕, Chúxī) — The most important night. Families gather for the reunion dinner (年夜飯). Staying up until midnight (守歲, shǒusuì) is practised to bid farewell to the old year.
  • Day 1 — Firecrackers at dawn. Wearing new clothes. Visiting elders and receiving red envelopes (紅包). Auspicious greetings exchanged.
  • Day 2 — Married daughters return to their parents' home (回娘家). Prayers to the God of Wealth.
  • Days 3-4 — Continuing family visits. In some traditions, Day 3 is considered unlucky for visiting to avoid arguments.
  • Day 5 — Welcoming the God of Wealth (財神, Cáishén). Firecrackers to attract prosperity. Businesses traditionally reopen.
  • Days 6-14 — Temple fairs (廟會), folk performances, lantern preparation. The festive atmosphere continues with community gatherings.
  • Day 15 — Lantern Festival (元宵節) — The grand finale. Lanterns displayed, lantern riddles (燈謎) solved. Families eat tangyuan (glutinous rice balls) symbolising togetherness. The first full moon of the new year.

Traditional Customs (Pre-Qing)

Spring Couplets (春聯) — Paired lines of auspicious poetry hung on doorframes. This literary tradition dates to the Five Dynasties period (907-960 CE), when they were carved on peach wood boards before evolving to red paper during the Ming Dynasty.

Door Gods (門神) — Painted guardian figures on gates, a custom established by the Tang Dynasty. The most famous pair are Qin Shubao and Yuchi Gong, generals of Emperor Taizong.

Paper Cutting (剪紙) — Intricate red paper designs depicting zodiac animals, flowers, and auspicious characters, displayed on windows to welcome the new year.

Red Envelopes (紅包) — Containing lucky money (壓歲錢, yāsuìqián, literally "money to suppress age"), given by elders to children as blessings for the coming year.

Firecrackers (鞭炮) — Originally bamboo thrown into fires (which cracked loudly when heated), later evolving into gunpowder firecrackers after the Tang Dynasty invention of black powder.

Festival Foods

Dumplings (餃子) — Shaped like ancient gold ingots (元寶), symbolising wealth. Families wrap them together on New Year's Eve. Dominant in northern China.

Nian Gao (年糕) — Sticky rice cake. The name is a homophone for "year higher" (年高), symbolising rising prosperity. Popular in southern and eastern China.

Fish (魚) — Always served at the reunion dinner. Yú is a homophone of 餘 (surplus), representing the wish for abundance.

Tang Yuan (湯圓) — Glutinous rice balls eaten during the Lantern Festival. Their round shape symbolises family unity and completeness. The name evokes 團圓 (tuányuán, reunion).

Temple Fairs and Performances

The lion dance and dragon dance date to the Han Dynasty. Temple fairs (廟會) feature folk performances, stilt walking, yangge dancing, and opera. These gatherings served essential social functions in traditional Chinese society, reinforcing community bonds and providing entertainment during the long winter festival.

The Lantern Festival (元宵節)

The Lantern Festival on the 15th day marks the first full moon of the new year. Tradition attributes its origins to Han Dynasty Emperor Ming, who ordered lanterns lit in palaces and temples to honour the Buddha. Lantern riddles (燈謎) — riddles written on lanterns for people to solve — became a beloved intellectual pastime by the Song Dynasty.

Spring Festival and the Zodiac

Each Chinese New Year ushers in a new zodiac animal. The incoming animal's personality is believed to colour the character of the entire year. People born in the same animal year enter their Ben Ming Nian (本命年) — their own zodiac year — which is traditionally considered a year of challenges requiring special precautions, most notably wearing red undergarments throughout the year.

Frequently Asked Questions

Chinese New Year falls on the second new moon after the winter solstice, between January 21 and February 20 on the Gregorian calendar. The date shifts yearly because of the lunisolar calendar system.

The festival spans 15 days, from New Year's Day to the Lantern Festival on the 15th of the first lunar month. New Year's Eve and the first five days are the most intensely celebrated.

Nian (年獸) is a mythical creature from Chinese folklore said to emerge on New Year's Eve. Its fear of red, loud noises, and fire gave rise to the festival's most iconic customs.

Red symbolises good fortune, joy, and vitality in Chinese culture. According to the Nian legend, the beast feared red, making it a protective colour. Red couplets, envelopes, lanterns, and clothing dominate the celebrations.

Chinese New Year is a specific cultural celebration on the Chinese lunisolar calendar. "Lunar New Year" is a broader term that includes similar celebrations in Korean (Seollal), Vietnamese (Tet), and other traditions. The dates typically coincide but customs differ.
